Ranking AI Slop on Google in 2026

Notes from NGMI Ep. 2 with Tony Yu and Indexsy. A fast, profane SEO/e-com chat on why AI content stopped being a moat, what actually moves rankings now, and a rapid-fire round calling each tactic NGMI or GMI.

The 30-second version

TL;DR

AI made written content a commodity, so quality is no longer the differentiator. The new tiebreaker Google leans on is domain authority, which is much harder to fake, so big sites win and small sites get crushed.

The winning play is "parasite SEO": publish on platforms Google already trusts (large publications, Facebook Groups, Reddit, Quora, Medium, LinkedIn), then push real traffic and click signals at it to make it rank. AI overview clicks are inching back up, but publishers are still down badly versus the old "position one gets ~30% of clicks" world.

Spamming AI articles on your own domain at high velocity gets you "clapped" by an algorithmic penalty you don't recover from. Slow, targeted, lightly-edited posting survives.

Key takeaways
Search landscape

AI overview clicks are recovering, but barely

Per Search Engine Land (Apr 24), click-through inside Google's AI experience ticked up roughly 1% to 2% since Dec 2025, with cited pages benefiting most. In absolute terms that looks like a big jump, but historically the top organic result earned ~30% of clicks, so publishers are still way down. The hosts frame AI overviews as "death to publishers, good for end users and brands", and here to stay.

Caveat

Your impression counts are inflated

Rising "search volume" isn't all human. Bots, scrapers, proxies, rank trackers, and even ChatGPT (which runs a search per query) all get counted. The takeaway: don't read too much into Search Console impressions. A chunk of that growth is machines, not buyers.

The core thesis

Content is commoditized, authority is the moat

The blunt claim: with AI, content quality "does not matter at all" as an algorithmic signal. Since everyone can generate competent text, Google falls back on what's harder to game, which is domain authority / size. Small sites are "completely crushed." Large trusted domains win by default.

The only differentiator from Google's point of view is domain authority, 'cause that's harder to game. That's the tiebreaker. It's no longer content.Indexsy (paraphrased)
The play

Parasite SEO: borrow someone else's authority

Instead of fighting to rank your own small domain, publish where Google already trusts the host: large publications (Indexsy's agency angle, with FTC disclosure), plus Facebook Groups, Reddit, Quora, Medium, and LinkedIn posts. The mechanics: get the post indexed, then build backlinks or push real click/CTR signals at it so Google reads it as going viral.

One concrete signal called out: Chrome usage data as a ranking input. Sending genuine traffic (e.g. your email list) to click a link can help lift it.

For operators

The 80/20 for a brand

If you run a store, you don't need the publication game. Spam content on your own blog and seed the "social" layer of Google: search every variant of your money keyword (e.g. "best waterproof shoes," "…for women"), drop genuinely helpful comments in the ranking discussions/forums, and get them upvoted. Scale it with VAs or software. It compounds hard across long-tail queries.

Attribution: it mostly shows up as organic/branded search, so it's tough to measure cleanly. Best proxy: track target keywords in Search Console, estimate clicks via CTR, and back into conversion using your own AdWords data for that keyword.

The warning

Spam your own domain and you get "clapped"

Blasting thousands of AI articles a month at your own URL produces a sharp Ahrefs spike (euphoria, goosebumps), then an algorithmic penalty about a week later that you never recover from. The penalty is algorithmic, not "looks like AI." Safer path: ~one targeted article a day, lightly human-edited, slower posting velocity. Once a domain is burned, buy/use a different one.

Rapid-fire: NGMI or GMI?

Spamming AI blog content on your own domain

NGMI

High-velocity blasting = a one-way ticket to an unrecoverable penalty. Targeted, slow, edited posting only.

ChatGPT / LLM shopping & e-commerce discovery

GMI

E-com through LLMs is going to work. The open question is the ad model: it has to be far better-targeted than Google Ads or trust (and engagement) collapses.

Starbucks "describe your mood / upload a photo" ChatGPT app

NGMI

"Crypto vibes." Not everything needs an AI-vibes layer. Better wins are behind-the-scenes (purchase-history push notifications, revenue optimization), not feeling-based drink picks.

Parasite SEO on trusted platforms

GMI

Still works, and arguably works better now that Google can't tell good content from bad and defaults to ranking large hosts.

Apple / Siri as a commerce layer

JURY OUT

Now open to any LLM and positioned to take rev-share on transactions. Big upside if Siri (and Alexa) actually start driving purchases. Hardware/timing dependent.

Monetizing a food blog for free meals

NGMI

"Selling your soul for a free meal." The better model is paid sponsored slots, or "eat with the host" guest spots with local-business plugs.

Glossary
NGMI / GMI: "Not Gonna Make It" / "Gonna Make It." The show's verdict shorthand for whether a tactic or bet will pay off.
Clapped / Clap City: hit with an algorithmic Google penalty. Rankings collapse with no recovery.
Parasite SEO: ranking content by hosting it on a high-authority third-party platform instead of your own domain.
AI overview: Google's AI-generated answer box that summarizes results above the organic links.
YMYL: "Your Money or Your Life" niches Google scrutinizes heavily. The hosts note AI overviews often don't fire in some of them.
