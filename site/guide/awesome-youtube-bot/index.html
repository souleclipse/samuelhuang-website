1What It Is & How It Works

Awesome YouTube Bot is a YouTube optimization tool that mimics real human viewing. Instead of blasting fake views through an API, it opens a real browser (or an Android emulator), navigates to your video the way a person would (via YouTube search, suggested videos, Google, direct, or an embed), watches it, scrolls, changes quality/speed, and can like, comment, and subscribe with logged-in Google accounts.

vs. SMM panels

Why "real browser" matters

SMM panels push 100k views in hours via unofficial APIs, with no browser, no user, no interaction. YouTube's AI spots and strips that. AYB is slower (it can't open 100k browsers at once) but each view is a real watch with a unique fingerprint, so views look organic and stick.

The levers

What it can move

Views & watch-time, likes, comments, subscriptions, live-stream views, and crucially keyword CTR: search your keyword, scroll, find your video, click, watch to the end. That search-find-click pattern is the YouTube ranking signal.

The five view sources

4Accounts & IPs: Get These Two Right

Almost every "it didn't work / my views dropped" problem traces to one of these two. Nail them before anything else.

Google accounts

  • Views: not strictly required, but accounts prevent drops. Per the latest YouTube changes, accounts are effectively needed for views to stick. Around 30 to 50 good accounts can support thousands of views.
  • Likes / comments / subscribes: mandatory. You can't engage as a guest. You need roughly one account per like/sub (1,000 likes is about 1,000 accounts).
  • Quality > quantity. Aged accounts with an avatar and watch history massively outperform fresh ones. Low-quality accounts are the #1 cause of drops.
  • Warm up weak accounts with a standalone Random Act job (Section 6). Let the bot watch random videos on different IPs to build history before you use them for real.
  • Cookies are stored after the first successful login, so the bot doesn't re-login every run. 2FA accounts are supported via a secret key; branded accounts (one Google account, many channels) via a branded: prefix.
Account import format · accounts.txt
email@gmail.com:password
email2@gmail.com:password2|recovery@email.com
# 2FA account — add the 32-digit secret key after the password
email3@gmail.com:password3|recovery@email.com|JBSWY3DPEHPK3PXP
# branded account (one login, multiple channels)
branded:email4@gmail.com:password4

IP changing

Same toolkit philosophy as ATB: residential IPs are what keep views alive. Blacklisted/free/datacenter proxies are the other big drop cause.

5Your First View Campaign, Step by Step

  1. Import accounts & set your IP

    Import your Google accounts (Section 4) and configure proxies/VPN on the left. For views that stick, tick "use accounts" and use residential IPs.

  2. Add a View job

    Import campaign → action View. Enter a keyword (your video's title works) and the video URL (trim to just the YouTube domain + video ID, drop extra parameters).

  3. Pick the view source

    Choose YouTube Search for keyword CTR (bot searches → scrolls → finds → clicks), or Suggested / Google / Direct / Embedded. YouTube Search and Suggested carry the most ranking weight.

  4. Set watch time, quality & speed

    Set a watch-time range (e.g. 60-90s). Watch at least 30-40 seconds, since YouTube ignores shorter views. Quality/speed can be random for realism; "default" picks data-saver to save proxy bandwidth.

  5. Set count & (optionally) Random Act

    Set how many views. Enable Random Act so the bot does natural activity (recommended/trending/short videos, random keyword searches, like/dislike) before the real view. Far more human.

  6. Choose browser or emulator, then Start

    Desktop = Chrome/Firefox; mobile = pick emulator (LD Player). Enable multi-threading to run several at once. Hit Start and watch the job panel / progress.

Bulk job import format · jobs.txt
# keyword|URL : action : viewtime-or-commentlist : count
my video title|https://youtu.be/VIDEOID:view:60-90:15
https://youtu.be/VIDEOID:like::2
https://youtu.be/VIDEOID:comment:MyCommentList:5
# no keyword? skip the pipe and start with the URL

6Likes, Comments, Subs & Nested Campaigns

Comments + spintax

Build a named comment list (one comment per line), then add a Comment job pointing at the video + list, and the bot picks one at random. Use spintax to multiply variety: {cool|interesting|amazing} makes the bot pick one word each time, so comments don't repeat.

Spintax comment
This is a really {cool|interesting|amazing} video, {thanks|appreciate it}!

Random Act (warm-up & realism)

Random Act makes the bot do natural things: browse recommended/trending/short videos, run random keyword searches (spintax supported), like/dislike. It runs either around a real job or as a standalone job. The standalone version is the go-to for warming up Google accounts: run it for hours on different IPs to build watch history before using those accounts for real engagement.

Nested campaigns (the realistic combo)

Real fans don't just view. They watch, then like, maybe subscribe, maybe comment, all in one sitting. Nested campaigns chain those into one browser session: View 20-30s → Like → View another minute → Subscribe → Comment. Set the count on the first job only (later jobs inherit it, so 100 first-job views give 100 likes). This single-session bundle is one of the strongest anti-detection patterns AYB offers.

Probability dials

You don't have to like/subscribe on every session. Set a like at 100% but a subscribe at, say, 35%, so engagement looks like real human variability rather than a perfect robot that subscribes every single time.

Source · "Awesome YouTube Bot" (comments/spintax, random act, nesting) + integration video

"Nested campaigns… you can have a view job, then like the video, another view job, then a subscribe job, and finally a comment job — all of them in just one browser at the same time… define the number on the first job." · "The standalone random act job is commonly used for warming up Google accounts." · Integration: "I set a task to like the video 100% of the time… subscribe to the channel with a 35% probability."

7Mobile Views & AI Mode

Mobile views via emulator

A large share of YouTube watch-time is mobile, so mobile views matter. AYB drives LD Player (or MEmu) to watch on a real Android environment, and each emulated device gets its own unique fingerprint. Set the view job up normally, then select emulator (not Firefox) before starting; enable VT in BIOS first. Multi-threading runs several emulator instances at once; pair with logged-in accounts to also prime them.

AI mode

Add an OpenAI (GPT-4o) API key in AI configuration and describe what the agent should do in plain language: open YouTube, handle cookie popups, watch realistically for N seconds, leave thoughtful comments, like, explore the channel, subscribe, hit the notification bell, do randomized actions. Run it before or after the main job with a timeout. It's especially good for warming up new Gmail accounts with believable, varied activity.

Source · "Android Phone Emulation in AYB" + "AYB's AI is here"

"Choose the emulator here, not Firefox… each Android device created has its own unique fingerprint… link this with logged-in Google accounts; this setup is great for priming your Gmail accounts." · AI: "instruct the AI agent to visit a YouTube video URL, handle cookie popups, watch realistically for set durations, leave authentic comments, like videos, subscribe and even activate notification bells… decide if the agent should run before or after the primary campaign action."

8★ The YouTube CTR & Ranking Playbook

YouTube ranks on satisfaction signals: did people search this, click it, watch a meaningful chunk, and engage? AYB lets you manufacture those, but the same realism rules decide whether it helps or backfires.

Watch-time and click-through beat raw view count. A real viewer searches, clicks, and stays. Engineer that, not a counter going up.

10,000 direct views that bounce at 5 seconds do nothing. 500 search-sourced views that watch 60%+ and occasionally like/sub can move a ranking.

1. Lead with keyword-CTR views

  • Use the YouTube Search source on the keywords you want to rank for: search, scroll, find, click, watch. That's the CTR signal that ties your video to a query.
  • Target keywords you can realistically rank for (lower competition, where your video already appears a few rows down). CTR nudges; it doesn't conjure rankings from nowhere.
  • Mix in Suggested source to signal relevance to related videos.

2. Maximize watch-time honestly

  • Always >30-40s, and ideally a real percentage of the video. Set watch-time as a range so it varies.
  • Don't watch 100% every time. Real audience retention is a curve, not a wall. Vary durations so some drop off early, some watch to the end.

3. Engage like humans, in proportion

  • Use nested campaigns so the same session that watches also (sometimes) likes/subscribes/comments. That's how real fans behave.
  • Keep ratios believable: likes < views, subscribes « views, comments rare. A video with more likes than views screams manipulation.
  • Spintax + varied comments so engagement never looks templated.

4. Quality of identity

  • Aged, warmed accounts + residential IPs. This is the whole ballgame for views sticking. Warm new accounts with standalone Random Act before using them.
  • Mix desktop + mobile (emulator) to mirror a real audience.
  • Ramp gradually. A new upload that jumps to thousands of views in an hour is the classic fake-out. Drip it over days using the Loop function and scheduling.

5. Combine with Awesome Traffic Bot

Chain an ATB Google-search task with an AYB YouTube task in one flow so a single "person" finds your web result and your video and engages with both, the multi-surface footprint real interest produces. See the Traffic Bot guide →

Footprints that get videos/channels flagged
  • Views with no accounts, or low-quality/blacklisted IPs (→ drops).
  • Likes > views, or subscribe-on-every-view.
  • All direct-source views, all identical watch-time.
  • Overnight view spikes on a fresh upload.
  • Templated, identical comments.

9Drops, Measuring & Risk

Why views drop (and how to stop it)

  • Bad IPs: blacklisted, low-quality, or free proxies. Use residential. #1 cause.
  • Weak / no Google accounts. Use aged accounts; warm fresh ones first.
  • Watch time under 30-40s. YouTube ignores those views entirely.

Measuring impact

Watch YouTube Studio: views, average view duration / percentage, traffic sources (you want "YouTube search" and "Suggested" rising), impressions CTR, and ranking for your target keywords (search them and see where your video sits). Treat it as an experiment: baseline, change one variable, watch a week or two, keep a control video.

Honest risk note, read this

Sending artificial views/engagement violates YouTube's Terms of Service and its fake-engagement policies; YouTube actively detects and removes it, and it can result in strikes or channel termination. Dalga itself strongly advises against using AYB on monetized videos. Artificial views on monetized content is an AdSense-invalid-traffic problem that can get a channel demonetized or banned. This guide documents a tool you license; it does not make the technique safe. Go gradual, keep ratios real, and never point it at a channel (especially a monetized one) you can't afford to lose.

Source · "Awesome YouTube Bot" (drops + tips) + product page

"The most common reason [for drops] is the proxy — if we use blacklisted IPs, low-quality proxies or free proxies, YouTube can detect them… if our Google accounts are low quality, or worse you send views without any Google accounts, you will see drops… viewing a video less than 30 to 40 seconds, YouTube ignores this kind of views." · Product page: "We strongly advise against using Awesome YouTube Bot on monetized videos."

10Settings Cheat Sheet

Non-negotiables

  • Watch time: > 30-40s, set as a range, vary it.
  • IPs: residential only. Blacklisted/free proxies = drops.
  • Accounts: aged + warmed. About 30 to 50 for thousands of views; roughly 1 per like/sub.
  • View source: YouTube Search (CTR) + Suggested for ranking. Direct = filler.

Copy-ready formats

  • Accounts: email:password|recovery|2FA-secret · branded: branded:email:password
  • Jobs: keyword|URL:action:time-or-list:count
  • Proxies: User:Pass@IP:Port · rotating add |API
  • Spintax: {cool|interesting|amazing}

The realistic-growth recipe

  • Nested session: view 60s+ → sometimes like (varied %) → rarely subscribe/comment.
  • Source = YouTube Search on a winnable keyword; add Suggested.
  • Aged accounts + residential IPs; warm new accounts with standalone Random Act first.
  • Mix desktop + emulator; ramp over days with Loop + scheduling.
  • Keep likes < views, subs « views; track in YouTube Studio.
  • Never on a monetized channel you care about.

11FAQ

Do I really need Google accounts?

For likes, comments and subscribes, yes, mandatory (you can't engage as a guest). For views they're not strictly required but they stop your views from dropping, so in practice you want them. Roughly one account per like/sub; about 30 to 50 good accounts can carry thousands of views.

Why are my views dropping?

Three usual suspects: blacklisted/free/low-quality proxies, weak or missing Google accounts, and watch time under 30-40 seconds. Fix all three: residential IPs, aged/warmed accounts, longer watch time.

Can it send 100,000 views in a few hours like an SMM panel?

No, and that's the point. It opens real browsers, so throughput is bounded, but the views are real watches with unique fingerprints that look organic and stick, unlike API-blasted panel views that YouTube strips.

Should I use it on a monetized channel?

Dalga explicitly advises against it, and so does common sense. Artificial views on monetized content is invalid traffic that can get a channel demonetized or terminated. Keep it off anything monetized you care about.

How do I do mobile views?

Install LD Player (the AYB installer can do it), enable Intel VT in BIOS, set up your view job, then choose emulator instead of Firefox before starting. Multi-threading runs several emulator instances at once.

What are nested campaigns and why use them?

They chain view → like → subscribe → comment into one browser session, like a real fan. Set the count on the first job; later jobs inherit it. It's one of the most natural, hardest-to-detect patterns AYB offers.

2FA or multi-channel accounts?

Both supported. For 2FA, append the 32-digit secret key after the password and the bot generates codes. For one Google account with multiple channels, prefix the entry with branded: and the bot switches between channels automatically.
